Some drugs are cheaper when paying out-of-pocket. getty I recently discovered that one of my medications would be more expensive with insurance than without it. Perplexed, I called the insurer to ask, 'How can this be?' Their response was 'That is a very good question.' Like most Americans, I assumed that paying more for insurance meant paying less for drugs. But soaring drug prices and the advent of prescription discount cards have, in some cases, turned this assumption on its head. The list price for my drug, which is a generic asthma medication, recently rose to nearly $4,000 a month and the co-pay — the portion of the drug's cost for which I was responsible — increased to more than $1,300 a month.
