Editorial: Don't let public officials hide from their constituents
mercurynews.com
news
2022-06-04 12:30:28

In the early 1950s, local elected officials often conducted public business in private. Members of a city council, for example, would hash out decisions in advance and then merely formalize them, often without discussion, at an open meeting.
News reports on this secretive behavior led Assemblyman Ralph M. Brown to author California's open-meeting statute for local government. The Brown Act, signed into law by Gov. Earl Warren in 1953, has repeatedly been amended since to address unanticipated issues and evolving technology.
