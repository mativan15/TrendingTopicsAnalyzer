A British View of the Steel Corporation

THE iron and steel industry, from mining the ore to putting the finest wire in a musical instrument, or from smelting the pig-iron to building a huge ship and her engines, constitutes a group of the most interesting and highly skilled occupations mankind has yet indulged in, and is, next to agriculture, the world's most important productive industry. Even in the United Kingdom, so famed for its cotton and woolen trades, the metal group of industries beat the textile group at the last census (1901), measured by the number of persons employed; while in wages paid, in skill required and displayed, in capital invested, in value of goods produced, and in general economic importance, iron stands out above all other manufacturing trades.
